Full Nutrition Framework

Macro targets built for a body that sits 10+ hours a day.

This isn't gym-bro nutrition. These are macro ranges specifically calculated for drivers managing blood pressure and weight while sitting most of the day, built around real truck stop food — not chicken breast and rice.

Daily targets · Adjust to your size

Your Daily Macro Range

Based on a 180–220 lb driver in a seated occupation aiming to maintain or lose weight while protecting blood pressure. Adjust down 10% if you're under 160 lbs, up 10% if over 240 lbs.

Roughly 2,000–2,200 calories per day. Protein is the priority macro — it preserves muscle and keeps you full longer between stops, which directly reduces snacking.
Sodium target · The #1 lever

Your Sodium Ceiling

Most truck stop meals contain 1,500–2,500mg of sodium in a single item. The American Heart Association recommends an ideal limit of 1,500mg per day for blood pressure management.

  • ✦Target: Under 2,000mg total sodium per day
  • ✦Reality check: One fast food combo meal can be 1,800–2,400mg alone
  • ✦The fix: One high-sodium meal per day, max. Build the other two around the swaps in this guide.
Macros matter less than consistency. Hit the range, not perfection.
Meal timing · Built around HOS

Eating Around Your Driving Windows

When you eat matters as much as what you eat. This timing strategy avoids the energy crash that hits most drivers around hour 5–6 of a shift.

  • 1Before driving (30 min prior): Protein + complex carb — eggs, oatmeal, or a protein bar. Avoid sugary pastries that spike and crash.
  • 2Hour 3–4 of shift: Small protein snack (nuts, jerky, string cheese) to prevent the energy dip before it happens.
  • 3Main meal at your break: Balanced plate — protein, vegetable or fruit, moderate carb. This is your 30-minute HOS break.
  • 4Evening meal, 2+ hours before sleep: Lighter, lower sodium. Heavy late meals disrupt sleep quality and raise overnight blood pressure.
If you're doing an overnight push, treat your "breakfast" as whatever meal follows your longest sleep block — your body's clock matters more than the actual clock on the wall.

Cab Stocking List

What to keep in your cab — and your restock day shopping list.

Half the battle is having the right food within reach before hunger makes the decision for you. Here's exactly what to buy on restock day at any Walmart Supercenter or truck stop store.

No refrigeration needed

Shelf-Stable Cab Stock

  • ✦Unsalted or lightly salted almonds, walnuts, or mixed nuts
  • ✦Protein bars under 10g sugar (check label — many are candy bars in disguise)
  • ✦Tuna or chicken packets (no can opener needed)
  • ✦Whole grain crackers
  • ✦Dried fruit, unsweetened (raisins, apricots)
  • ✦Oatmeal cups (just add hot water from any truck stop)
  • ✦Sugar-free electrolyte packets
Cooler section (12V cooler or insulated bag)

Cold Stock

  • ✦Hard-boiled eggs (pre-boil a dozen on restock day)
  • ✦String cheese or cheese sticks
  • ✦Greek yogurt cups (high protein, low sugar varieties)
  • ✦Pre-cut vegetables (baby carrots, celery, cucumber)
  • ✦Fresh fruit: apples, oranges (they travel well)
  • ✦Rotisserie chicken, portioned into containers

Cab-Friendly Fitness Program

A structured 4-week program. Zero equipment. Zero gym.

Each session is under 20 minutes and built specifically for HOS breaks. This is the expanded version of the Parking Lot Protocol from the free guide — with weekly progression.

Week 1–2 · Foundation

Building the Habit

  • ✦3 laps around your truck (walk) at every fuel stop
  • ✦10 squats + 10 push-ups against your rig
  • ✦30-second hip flexor stretch each side
  • ✦Goal: consistency, not intensity. Do it every single stop.
Week 3–4 · Progression

Building Real Strength

  • ✦4 laps around your truck
  • ✦15 squats + 15 push-ups + 10 lunges each leg
  • ✦20 calf raises + 30-second plank against your bumper
  • ✦Hip flexor + hamstring stretch, 45 seconds each side
By week 4, most drivers report noticeably less back pain and better energy through the afternoon slump.
500+ reps a week from stops alone changes a body over time.
Sleep Apnea Self-Assessment

Know your risk before your DOT examiner does.

Sleep apnea is the #2 reason drivers fail or receive a conditional DOT certificate. This structured self-check helps you understand your risk level before you're in the exam chair.

The STOP-BANG Risk Check

This is a simplified version of the clinical screening tool used by sleep specialists. Count how many apply to you.

  • ⚠Snoring loudly (louder than talking, heard through walls)
  • ⚠Tired during the day despite adequate sleep hours
  • ⚠Observed stopping breathing during sleep (ask a partner or rider)
  • ⚠Pressure — high blood pressure, treated or untreated
  • ⚠BMI over 35
  • ⚠Age over 50
  • ⚠Neck circumference over 17in (men) / 16in (women)
  • ⚠Gender — male sex (higher baseline risk)
0–2 yes answers: low risk. 3–4: intermediate risk — mention it to your doctor. 5+: high risk — talk to your doctor before your next DOT physical, not after. This tool does not diagnose sleep apnea; only a sleep study can do that. It tells you whether the conversation with your doctor is worth having now.
Bonus Section

Truck stop hygiene, cleanliness, and health protection.

Health on the road is not only about food and fitness. Showers, restrooms, laundry, skin care, and basic cleanliness matter — especially when you live and work in shared public spaces.

Truck stop showers

Shower Do’s

  • ✓Wear shower shoes every time.
  • ✓Bring your own towel when possible.
  • ✓Use a waterproof bag for soap, razor, toothbrush, and grooming items.
  • ✓Dry your feet completely before putting socks back on.
  • ✓Keep a backup hygiene kit in the cab for delays.
Avoid this

Shower Don’ts

  • ×Do not walk barefoot in public showers.
  • ×Do not place clean clothes directly on the floor or bench.
  • ×Do not reuse damp towels for multiple days.
  • ×Do not ignore athlete’s foot, rashes, or skin irritation.
Restrooms & public spaces

Restroom Cleanliness Tips

  • ✓Carry hand sanitizer, wipes, and backup tissue.
  • ✓Wash hands before eating, after fueling, and after touching pumps or door handles.
  • ✓Keep disinfecting wipes in the cab for steering wheel, handles, phone, and dashboard.
  • ✓Use separate bags for dirty clothes, shower gear, and clean clothing.
For men and women

Personal Hygiene Kit

  • •Shower shoes
  • •Body wipes
  • •Hand sanitizer
  • •Disinfecting wipes
  • •Deodorant
  • •Clean socks and underwear
  • •Small laundry bag
  • •Toothbrush, toothpaste, floss
  • •Moisturizer or skin barrier cream
  • •Menstrual products if needed
Cab cleanliness

Cab Health Rules

  • 1Wipe high-touch surfaces at least twice per week.
  • 2Keep food trash out of the cab overnight when possible.
  • 3Separate clean clothes from worn clothes.
  • 4Change pillowcase or sleep towel weekly.
  • 5Air out shoes and damp gear to prevent odor, fungus, and skin issues.
Hygiene is health protection. Skin infections, stomach bugs, foot fungus, odor, and poor sleep can all start with small cleanliness habits slipping during long runs.
